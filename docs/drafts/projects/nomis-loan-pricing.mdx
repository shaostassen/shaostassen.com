## Problem

Loan pricing is a joint prediction-and-optimization problem: for a given applicant and rate, what's the probability the loan is taken (and performs), and what rate maximizes expected revenue across the portfolio without pricing good customers away? [CONFIRM: was the 0.903 AUC on take-up/conversion, default risk, or something else?]

## Constraints

- Real lending data: imbalanced outcomes, selection bias (you only observe outcomes for loans that happened), and drift as market rates move.
- Model decisions affect real prices, so interpretability and sanity constraints matter — a model that prices non-monotonically in risk is a liability regardless of its AUC. [CONFIRM: whether monotonicity/regulatory constraints applied.]

## Approach

Gradient-boosted trees for the outcome model, feeding a pricing layer that translates predicted probabilities into expected-revenue estimates across candidate rates. [CONFIRM: your specific contribution — feature engineering, model development, the lift analysis, or the full path.]

## Why it's technically hard

The modeling is entangled with economics. Price sensitivity is the quantity you actually need, but it's confounded in observational data — customers who got high rates differ systematically from those who got low ones. And "0.903 AUC" only matters if it survives out-of-time validation, because a lending model evaluated on a random split of historical data flatters itself.

## Problem

Combat robotics is almost universally teleoperated — a human closes the loop. Huey's goal was to close it in software: detect the opponent, orient toward it, and drive the engagement autonomously, in a domain where the environment is actively trying to break your sensors.

## Constraints

- A 3 lb total weight budget shared between armor, weapon, drivetrain, battery, *and* the compute and camera for autonomy — every gram of perception hardware is a gram not spent on surviving hits.
- Match-length decision latency: hesitation loses fights.
- Arena conditions are hostile to vision — harsh lighting, debris, motion blur, and an opponent whose appearance changes as it takes damage.

## Why it's technically hard

Everything is adversarial and non-stationary. The target detector's subject is trying not to be where you predict; impacts physically knock sensors out of calibration mid-match; and the controller has to handle the target vanishing from frame constantly — which is exactly why anti-windup matters, since a naive integrator saturates during every detection gap and then slams the robot past the target when it reacquires. The whole loop has to run within a weight and power budget that a phone would find restrictive.
